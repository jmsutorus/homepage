Forest Floor Wild Mushroom Risotto

The Harvest

  • Wild Mushroom Mix

    500g Chanterelles, Porcini, and Morel

  • Arborio Rice

    300g premium short-grain

  • Mushroom Stock

    1.5 Liters, warmed

  • Shallots & Garlic

    2 shallots, 3 cloves garlic, finely minced

  • Dry White Wine

    150ml Pinot Grigio or Sauvignon Blanc

  • Parmigiano-Reggiano

    100g freshly grated

  • Unsalted Butter & Thyme

    50g butter, 4 sprigs fresh thyme

Preparation Method

1

Sauté the Woodland Treasures

Clean the mushrooms carefully using a brush (avoid water if possible). Heat a large heavy-bottomed pan with a tablespoon of olive oil. Add the mushrooms and sauté until they release their moisture and turn a deep golden brown. Season with salt and pepper, then set aside.

2

Build the Foundation

In the same pan, melt half the butter. Add the finely minced shallots and cook until translucent. Add the garlic and arborio rice, stirring constantly for 2 minutes until the edges of the rice grains are transparent—a process known as 'tostatura' that ensures a creamy result.

3

Deglaze & Temper

Pour in the white wine and stir until completely absorbed by the rice. Begin adding the warm mushroom stock, one ladle at a time. Stir frequently, allowing the rice to absorb the liquid before adding the next ladle. This agitation releases the starches, creating the signature risotto silkiness.

4

The Mantecatura

Once the rice is al dente, fold in the sautéed mushrooms and fresh thyme. Remove from heat. Add the remaining butter and freshly grated Parmigiano-Reggiano. Cover for 2 minutes, then stir vigorously to achieve the perfect 'all'onda' (wavy) consistency. Serve immediately.

Wine Pairing

A classic **Nebbiolo** or an earthy **Pinot Noir** from the Willamette Valley. The forest floor notes in the wine perfectly mirror the umami profile of the wild mushrooms.

Elena's Secret

Infuse your mushroom stock with a single dried kombu sheet while heating. It subtly boosts the glutamate levels for a deeper, richer flavor without overpowering the fungi.
